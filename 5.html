A Cool Discovery in Isesaki!

Big Strange Hills and Clay Figures

When I first came to Japan almost 20 years ago, I didn’t know much about Japanese history. One day, I walked through a park and found a big, strange-looking hill. I later learned it was called a kofun—-an ancient古代の tomb墓 built around the 3rd century! Kofun means “old tomb” in Japanese. These large mounds were made to honor(人を) 敬う important people after they died.

My curiosity好奇心 led me to learn more, and soon I discovered発見 the fascinating world of haniwa.

Haniwa are terracotta clay figures像 that were placed on or around kofun. At first, they were just simple shapes, like tubes. But over time, they changed into shapes like houses, animals, warriors, and even chickens! People believed haniwa helped protect the spirits精神 of the dead and kept遠ざける evil away.

A Puzzling Haniwa Figure

This year, I visited the Akabori Museum of History and Folklore here in Isesaki. I was amazed to see some big haniwa! Now here’s the exciting part—an amazing discovery happened recently!

A long time ago, part of a rooster-shaped haniwa was found in a tomb called the Akabori Chausuyama Tumulus, near Mt. Akagi. That piece was sent to the Tokyo National Museum and kept there for over 80 years.

Then, at a different place in Isesaki called the Kama-no-kuchi Site, where haniwa were once made, people found another piece from a wing of a rooster-shaped haniwa!

When they looked closely, they realized実現する it was a perfectぴったり合う fit with the museum’s piece! It was like putting together a puzzle and finding the missing part after all this time. Wow!

Mr. Toru Kawamichi, a passionate情熱的な expert at the Akabori Museum said, “It gave me goosebumps鳥肌が立つ!” (not chickenbumps鳥肌).

The rooster haniwa has a special meaning. His foot looks like it holds a branch枝, showing it’s ready for the rest, for the night. Roosters are symbols of the sunrise, but as they are made as haniwa, their meaning is to look for the dawn夜明け, for the night’s darkness. Also, a tiny claw爪 on the back of its leg proves it’s a rooster, not a hen!

Bringing the pieces back together from these two places—the Chausuyama site and the tomb in Akabori Village, with the previous one from the Tokyo museum—took careful work by many people. But it helped us understand the history better.

Beyond Just History

And guess what? Haniwa aren’t just old history—they’re also part of modern culture! You’ll find haniwa in video games, anime, and even children’s shows like Kirby, Animal Crossing, and Anpanman.

More than just a historical curiosity, it’s a powerful reminderリマインダー of how objects from the past still have stories to tell.

So next time you're in Isesaki, don’t miss the chance to visit the Akabori Museum of History and Folklore. Who knows—you might discover a new piece of the past.

You might see the famous rooster haniwa—or maybe even discover your own piece of the past!
